## Section 1 — About this site

**What is this site?**

An information hub about hantavirus — what it is, where it's found, and what's happening with the current outbreak. The map shows endemic regions globally, major historical outbreaks, the route of the current MV Hondius cruise outbreak, and active case locations marked with pulsing markers. The updates feed pulls fresh hantavirus-related news from official health agencies and major news outlets several times a day.

**Where does the data come from?**

Three layers. The cruise route and the dispersal data are based on official WHO, CDC, and ECDC reports plus reporting from CNN, BBC, Al Jazeera, ABC News, CBS, NPR, Reuters, and PBS. The live news feed pulls directly from the public RSS feeds of these sources. The case and death counts come from WHO Disease Outbreak News bulletins.

**How often does the site update?**

News headlines refresh every 6 hours. Case counts and official agency updates refresh every 12 hours. The cruise route itself is fixed history and doesn't change.

**Is this site affiliated with any health agency or news outlet?**

No. This is an independent tracker built to make scattered information easier to follow. Links go directly to the original sources — we don't republish full articles, only headlines with links.

**Can I trust the case counts?**

The counts shown match the latest WHO Disease Outbreak News bulletin. If WHO updates its numbers, this site updates within 12 hours. We don't speculate beyond what's been officially reported.

**How do I report an error?**

If something looks wrong, email the contact address in the footer. We treat factual corrections seriously and update fast.

**Will this site keep running after the outbreak ends?**

Yes. Once the active phase is over, the site becomes a permanent historical record of the outbreak with an archive of news coverage, a timeline of events, and a science section explaining what happened and why it mattered.

## Section 2 — About the virus

**What is hantavirus?**

A family of viruses spread by rodents. Different strains exist on different continents. Most cause one of two illnesses in humans — hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) or hemorrhagic fever with renal syndrome (HFRS). The strain on the MV Hondius is Andes orthohantavirus, which causes HPS.

**How do you catch it?**

Almost always by inhaling dust contaminated with rodent urine, droppings, or saliva, usually in an enclosed space like a cabin or shed where infested droppings have dried and become airborne. Direct rodent bites can also transmit it.

**Why is this outbreak spreading person to person?**

The Andes virus is the only hantavirus strain ever documented to spread directly between humans. It's still rare — the main route is rodent to human — but it has been seen in family clusters and healthcare settings before. The MV Hondius is the largest such cluster in over a decade.

**What are the symptoms?**

A flu-like first phase: fever, headache, muscle aches, sometimes nausea. This lasts 4 to 10 days. Then a sudden second phase where fluid builds up in the lungs and breathing becomes very difficult. The second phase is where most deaths occur.

**How deadly is it?**

The case fatality rate for HPS caused by Andes virus is 35 to 40 percent. There is no specific antiviral treatment. Care is supportive — oxygen, fluids, ventilator support.

**How worried should I be if I've never been on a cruise ship?**

Very low concern. The outbreak is contained to passengers and crew of one ship and their close contacts. Andes virus is rare outside southern South America. Casual contact with anyone outside the affected group is not a transmission risk.

**Is there a vaccine?**

No vaccine is currently approved for hantavirus in most countries. Research has been ongoing for decades but no broadly approved vaccine exists. The Argentine and Chilean governments have funded some research given how common Andes virus is in those regions.

**Could this become a pandemic?**

According to WHO's current assessment, no. The virus does not spread efficiently between people in normal circumstances — it requires close, sustained contact with someone who is symptomatic. The cruise ship situation was unusual because of the closed environment and long shared exposure.

## Section 3 — Other questions

**Is it still safe to take a cruise?**

The risk of catching hantavirus on a cruise is, statistically, extremely low. The MV Hondius outbreak is unprecedented. That said, expedition cruises to remote regions carry different risk profiles than mainstream cruises — fewer medical resources, longer evacuation times, more wildlife contact. If you're considering an expedition cruise, talk to your doctor about region-specific health risks.

**What is the cruise operator saying?**

Oceanwide Expeditions, which operates the MV Hondius, has issued press updates on its website at oceanwide-expeditions.com. The company is cooperating with WHO, CDC, ECDC, and the relevant national authorities.

**Has anything like this happened before?**

Not on this scale, not on a cruise ship. Past Andes virus person-to-person clusters were in southern Argentina and involved family members or healthcare workers. The closest historical parallel for "outbreak on a confined vessel" is the various norovirus outbreaks that have hit cruise ships over the years — but norovirus is not life-threatening to healthy adults, and hantavirus is.

**Why is this getting so much news coverage?**

A combination of factors: the case fatality rate of the virus, the international passenger list, the remote and dramatic route, person-to-person spread of a rare virus, and the fact that it unfolded in real time as the ship was at sea with limited communication.

**Who built this site and why?**

This site was built independently to make scattered reporting easier to follow in one place. We're not affiliated with any agency, news outlet, or company. Contact details are in the footer.

**How can I help?**

If you find errors, report them. If you have access to official documents or news in languages we may have missed, send them. If you know someone affected by the outbreak, point them to the relevant national health authority — links are in the footer.
