## Homepage hero

Hantavirus is a family of rodent-borne viruses that have caused outbreaks across the Americas, Asia, and Europe for over 70 years. Most years, a few hundred cases are reported worldwide — usually in remote rural areas where people come into contact with infected rodent droppings. The illness is rare but serious, with case fatality rates ranging from about 1 percent in some strains to over 35 percent in others.

This site is a hub for understanding hantavirus and following current news. The most recent outbreak — the 2026 MV Hondius cruise ship cluster — is the largest documented person-to-person Andes virus event in over a decade and is featured below as the current active story. Use the map to explore where hantavirus is endemic, where it has caused historical outbreaks, where the current outbreak has traveled, and where active cases are being treated right now.

## Hantavirus in context

The hantavirus story didn't start with the MV Hondius. The first hantavirus was isolated in 1976 from a field mouse near the Hantan River in South Korea, though the disease it caused — Korean hemorrhagic fever — had been killing United Nations soldiers in Korea since the early 1950s. In 1993, an outbreak in the Four Corners region of the southwestern United States killed more than half its initial victims and led to the discovery of Sin Nombre virus. Andes virus, the strain involved in the current outbreak, was first identified in Argentina in 1995 and remains the only hantavirus known to spread person to person.

Today, hantavirus is endemic in the Americas (Sin Nombre in the US and Canada, Andes in Argentina and Chile, Choclo in Panama, and others), East Asia (Hantaan and Seoul viruses in China, Korea, Russia), and parts of Europe (Puumala in Scandinavia, Dobrava-Belgrade in the Balkans). Most years see a few hundred cases globally — never zero, rarely thousands.

## Notable past outbreaks

**Korean War, 1951–1954.** Thousands of UN soldiers fell ill with what was then called Korean hemorrhagic fever. Roughly 10 percent died. The virus behind it wasn't identified for another two decades.

**Four Corners, USA, 1993.** A sudden cluster of unexplained respiratory deaths in young, otherwise healthy Native American adults led to the rapid identification of Sin Nombre virus. The case fatality rate in the initial outbreak was over 50 percent. Sin Nombre remains the dominant hantavirus in North America.

**Argentina, El Bolsón, 1996.** The first documented case of person-to-person hantavirus transmission. Twenty cases, sixteen deaths, all linked to a small Patagonian town. This is what established Andes virus as the lone exception to the rodent-only rule.

**Yosemite National Park, USA, 2012.** Ten visitors who had stayed in tent cabins contracted Sin Nombre virus; three died. The outbreak was traced to mouse infestations in the cabin insulation.

## Current outbreak: MV Hondius cruise ship

### Stop 1 — Ushuaia, Argentina · April 1

The voyage began on April 1, 2026 from Ushuaia — the southernmost city in the world, often called the gateway to Antarctica. 114 guests boarded the MV Hondius for what was advertised as a standard South Atlantic expedition cruise. No one on board knew the trip would end up making global news.

### Stop 2 — Antarctic waters · April 3–8

The ship sailed south through the Drake Passage to the Antarctic Peninsula. Standard expedition activity: zodiac landings, wildlife viewing, glacier excursions. The first traces of what would become the outbreak were almost certainly already on board.

### Patient zero · April 6

Five days into the voyage, a Dutch passenger developed fever, headache, and mild diarrhea. The ship's medical team treated him as a routine illness. The Andes hantavirus has an incubation period of 7 to 39 days, meaning he was likely exposed before boarding or in the first days of the cruise.

### Stop 3 — South Georgia · April 8–11

The ship docked at Grytviken and surrounding sites on South Georgia, one of the most remote inhabited places on Earth. Passengers went ashore among penguin colonies, elephant seals, and the rusted remains of the old whaling station.

### First death · April 11

Patient zero died on board the MV Hondius, five days after first showing symptoms. The cause of death was not yet known to be hantavirus. The ship continued its northward route.

### Stop 4 — Tristan da Cunha · April 13–15

The ship reached Tristan da Cunha, the most remote inhabited island in the world — population just under 300. The MV Hondius spent two days there. Six additional guests boarded on April 15.

### Stop 5 — Saint Helena · April 21–24

The body of the deceased Dutch passenger was removed from the ship at Jamestown, Saint Helena, on April 24. His wife, who had also fallen ill, was disembarked here and evacuated to Johannesburg. 32 guests in total left the ship at this stop. The remaining passengers and crew continued north.

### Second death · Johannesburg · April 26

The wife of patient zero died in a Johannesburg hospital two days after being evacuated from Saint Helena. This was the second death linked to the outbreak, and the first signal that something more serious than a routine shipboard illness was unfolding.

### Stop 6 — Ascension Island · April 27

The ship departed Ascension Island. By this point, more passengers and crew were showing symptoms. The medical team began treating the outbreak as an undiagnosed cluster.

### WHO notified · May 2

The World Health Organization received notification of a cluster of severe acute respiratory illness aboard a cruise ship in the Atlantic Ocean. Andes hantavirus was confirmed by laboratory testing in the days that followed.

### Stop 7 — Praia, Cape Verde · May 3

The ship docked briefly at Praia, the capital of Cape Verde, en route to its final destination. The diagnosis was now public and emergency response coordination was underway across multiple countries.

### Stop 8 — Tenerife, Canary Islands · May 10

MV Hondius reached the Port of Granadilla in Tenerife on May 10, 2026. Evacuation began immediately. Spanish nationals were flown to Madrid, French nationals to Paris, Canadians to Quebec and on to British Columbia, Americans to Omaha and Atlanta. The third death had been recorded by this date.

### Where things stand

The outbreak is contained to the ship and known contacts. Person-to-person transmission of Andes hantavirus is rare but documented, and is what makes this outbreak unusual. WHO continues to assess the risk to the general public as low. Quarantine and monitoring are underway in six countries. Active cases — those still under medical care — are shown as pulsing markers on the map.
